# 4. Where India is, in numbers

This section establishes the FY 2024-25 cross-state descriptive landscape that the rest of the analysis works against. The figures and tables here are the empirical baseline — the levels — before we get to either the time trend (§5) or the regression results (§6 and §7).

## The state ranking

Figure 2 ranks the 36 states and union territories by their average monthly per-capita UPI transactions over FY 2024-25, coloured by income quartile. Telangana sits at the top (23.0 monthly transactions per person), followed by Chandigarh (21.3), Delhi (20.2), Goa (19.4), and Karnataka (17.1). The bottom of the distribution is dominated by the small north-eastern states and the most populous low-income states: Tripura (2.7), Manipur (3.0), Meghalaya (3.3), Bihar (3.7), Nagaland (4.2), and Uttar Pradesh (4.2). The India population-weighted average — the figure a national reader would normally cite — is 8.1 transactions per person per month, marked on the figure by the dashed vertical line.

The income-tier colouring makes the cross-state pattern visible at a glance. The upper half of the ranking is overwhelmingly the dark blue of the upper income quartile, and the lower half is overwhelmingly the dark red of the lowest quartile. There are exceptions in both directions. Sikkim (high income, mid-ranking UPI use) and Himachal Pradesh (upper-middle income, low UPI use) sit lower than their income tier alone would predict; Andhra Pradesh (mid-income, high UPI use) sits higher. But the headline is the strong association between income tier and per-capita UPI use, which is exactly what the regression in §7 will quantify.

## The summary statistics

Table 1 summarises the seven variables that enter the FY 2024-25 cross-section: per-capita UPI use (the dependent variable), the five regression controls (per-capita NSDP, internet density, urban share, PMJDY beneficiaries per adult, bank-office density), and the appendix-only literacy variable. For each variable we report the cross-state mean, standard deviation, minimum and maximum, and the population-weighted India-wide value.

Three patterns in the table are worth pulling out, because they illustrate how cross-state averages can diverge from population-weighted national figures and what that divergence reveals.

*Per-capita UPI use* has a state mean of 8.8 monthly transactions per person but a population-weighted India figure of 8.1. The gap is small but goes in a specific direction: the highest-use states are mostly *not* the most populous ones. Telangana, Chandigarh, Delhi, and Goa are smaller in population than their UPI rank would suggest, while Uttar Pradesh and Bihar — large and populous — sit toward the bottom. The state mean overstates the typical Indian's per-capita UPI use; the population-weighted figure is the more honest national headline.

*PMJDY beneficiaries per adult* shows the opposite pattern. The state mean is 0.40 accounts per adult, but the population-weighted figure is 0.55. The difference is large and reflects exactly what PMJDY's targeting was designed to do: enrollment is highest in populous low-income states (Bihar at 0.78, Madhya Pradesh at 0.70, Uttar Pradesh at 0.60, Rajasthan at 0.63, Jharkhand at 0.70). When we weight by population, those states pull the national figure up well above the cross-state mean. The same scheme that looks modest as a state-level mean (40 percent of adults) is a much larger national effort when measured in adult-weighted terms (55 percent of adults).

*Bank-office density per 100,000 population* shows the same direction as per-capita UPI use: state mean of 17.7 per 100k, population-weighted India figure of 12.3. Small states and union territories have many more bank offices per person than large states do (Goa, Sikkim, Himachal Pradesh, Chandigarh, Lakshadweep all sit above 30 offices per 100k; the largest states sit between 8 and 12). The state-mean figure should not be mistaken for the average Indian's access to a bank office — it overstates by close to 50 percent.

The remaining variables move less between state mean and population-weighted figure because they are more uniformly distributed: urban share (45.7 percent state mean, 35.7 percent population-weighted; the population-weighted figure pulls down because rural states are populous), literacy (85.6 vs 80.8 percent), internet density (80.4 vs 68.6 subscribers per 100), per-capita NSDP (₹146,000 vs ₹116,000).

## Reading these levels into the rest of the paper

Two reads of the descriptive picture set up what follows.

First, the *level* of cross-state variation in per-capita UPI use is large. An 8.5-fold gap between the lowest- and highest-use states is not a rounding error around the national average. Whatever the convergence trend looks like in §5, it is operating against an initial dispersion of meaningful magnitude.

Second, the cross-state association between income and UPI use that Figure 2 makes visible is not the only association the data contain. The contrasts visible in Table 1 — the population-weighted PMJDY figure being well above the state mean, the bank-office density figure showing the opposite pattern — preview the substantive distinction the regression will make between financial-inclusion programs (which target the population-heavy poorer states by design) and financial-inclusion infrastructure (which scales with state size and income). §6 and §7 do the formal version of this distinction.
