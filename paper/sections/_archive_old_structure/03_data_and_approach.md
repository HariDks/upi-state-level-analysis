# 3. Data and approach

This section describes the sources, the state-level scope, and the methodology shared across Parts I and II. We anchor each fiscal year's variables to data published as close to the analytical period as possible, log-transform every continuous variable so coefficients are elasticities, and cluster standard errors at the state level. The full data-construction trail is in Appendix B.

## Dependent variables

Parts I and II use definitionally different dependent variables. We do not chain them.

**Part I uses a five-rail digital-payment composite.** State-disaggregated digital-payment volumes for FY 2019-20 and FY 2020-21 are published by Indiastat from Lok Sabha Unstarred Question No. 1425 (28 July 2021). The variable is the sum of transactions across BHIM, IMPS, RuPay-on-POS, UPI, and USSD, reported as an annual count by state. Pure UPI state-level data does not exist for this period. UPI was already the largest of the five rails by FY 2020-21, so the composite was UPI-heavy in practice — but it is not the same variable as Part II's dependent variable.

**Part II uses pure UPI volume.** Indiastat publishes NPCI state-wise UPI transaction counts and values monthly from April 2023. We have 34 monthly observations per state through January 2026, which we aggregate into three twelve-month windows: April 2023 – March 2024, April 2024 – March 2025, and a rolling February 2025 – January 2026. The third window is rolling rather than fiscal-year-aligned because monthly data through March 2026 is not yet published; treating it as a twelve-month average preserves the same window length across observations.

Both DVs are normalised by population, giving per-capita transaction counts. The denominator is the mid-period mean of two adjacent 1-July populations from the Ministry of Health and Family Welfare projection report — for example, the FY 2024-25 denominator is the mean of the 1-July-2024 and 1-July-2025 populations. This places the denominator near the mid-point of the relevant fiscal year and avoids using a single boundary year for an annual variable.

A footnote on national totals: NPCI's published all-India UPI volume exceeds the sum of state-disaggregated counts because a meaningful share of transactions are reported under "Unclassified" — they cannot be attributed to a specific state. By January 2026 the Unclassified share approached forty percent of national volume. For the cross-state analysis the relevant question is whether the Unclassified bucket is randomly distributed across states or systematically over-represents particular ones. We tested this and find no statistical relationship between a state's Unclassified-share-loss and its income tier. The cross-state convergence patterns documented in §5 are robust to the bucket. Full diagnostic in Appendix B.

## Controls

The headline regressions use four state-level controls common to both eras (per-capita NSDP, urban share, PMJDY beneficiaries per adult, bank-office density per 100,000 population) plus one additional control available only in Part II (internet density). A sixth candidate — literacy — is excluded from both headlines for measurement-validity reasons documented in §9.

**Per-capita Net State Domestic Product** comes from Indiastat compilations of MoSPI state-account data, expressed at constant 2011-12 prices. Coverage varies by state and year: most states report final estimates for FY 2022-23, advance estimates for FY 2023-24, and a smaller subset for FY 2024-25. Where the latest fiscal year is unavailable for a state, the build script falls back to the most recent prior FY (most often FY 2023-24, occasionally FY 2022-23). The fallback affects the FY 2024-25 anchor — three states (Lakshadweep, Ladakh, the merged Dadra and Nagar Haveli and Daman and Diu) lack NSDP entirely and are dropped from the regression sample.

**Urban population share** is constructed by linear interpolation between the 2011 and 2021 census anchors of the urban–total population ratio, interpolated to the midpoint year of each analytical window. The 2021 anchor is itself a published projection rather than an enumerated census (the decennial census was deferred; the projection is from the same MoHFW report that provides population). Two small union territories — Lakshadweep and Chandigarh — already had urban shares at or near 100 percent in 2021, and the interpolation produces fractional values slightly above 1.0 in the post-2021 windows. We cap these at 1.0; the cap affects two state-window observations and shifts no coefficient materially.

**PMJDY beneficiaries per adult** is the cumulative count of basic savings accounts opened under the Pradhan Mantri Jan Dhan Yojana scheme (launched August 2014), normalised by adult population. The numerator is captured from snapshots of the Department of Financial Services portal page `pmjdy.gov.in/statewise-statistics` archived on the Internet Archive's Wayback Machine. The five snapshots used — 17 October 2019, 5 March 2021, 27 February 2024, 15 February 2025, and 13 December 2025 — are each within a few months of the relevant analytical anchor. The denominator is adult population, derived from the PLFS 2023-24 Annual Report sample composition (Table 1, persons aged 15 and above as a share of the total survey sample). PLFS 2023-24 is the only adult-share round we use; the Indian adult-share moves slowly, so applying it to all five anchors introduces only small approximation error.

**Bank-office density per 100,000 population** is the count of "functioning offices" of scheduled commercial banks reported by the Reserve Bank of India in Other STRBI Table 13 (DBIE), normalised by mid-period population. "Functioning offices" includes branches plus administrative offices and extension counters, across public, private, foreign, regional rural, small finance, and payments banks. We use end-of-FY snapshots: 31 March 2020 (Part I W1), 31 March 2021 (Part I W2), 31 March 2024 (Part II W1), 31 March 2025 (Part II W2), and 31 December 2025 (Part II W3 rolling-window end).

**Internet density** (Part II only) is internet subscribers per 100 population from the TRAI Yearly Performance Indicators report. The variable is available at the state level on a comparable basis from 31 March 2024 onwards. Part II uses the 31-Mar-2024 snapshot for W1 and the 31-Mar-2025 snapshot for W2 and W3 (the latest available). Pre-2023 TRAI data is reported by Licensed Service Areas which combine post-split states; this is why internet density does not appear in Part I.

## State scope and reconciliation

We treat 36 state and union-territory units as the universe of the analysis. The Series 1 source for Part I reports Jammu and Kashmir as a single unit; consistent with the convention used throughout Part I, we treat J&K as one unit and Ladakh is therefore absent from the Part I sample (in any case Ladakh did not exist as a separate UT until October 2019, mid-way through the first analytical year). For Part II, J&K and Ladakh are reported as separate units in the source data and are treated as separate units. The merged Dadra and Nagar Haveli and Daman and Diu (the two pre-2020 UTs were combined in January 2020) is treated as the post-merger single unit throughout, with pre-merger figures from any source aggregated to the merged canonical.

State names are spelled inconsistently across sources. Every ingestion script reconciles incoming names through a single canonical lookup table; unrecognised spellings cause the ingestion to fail loudly rather than silently drop or fuzzy-match. The lookup table records every spelling encountered, preserving an audit trail of source-by-source variation. Full canonicalisation rules are in Appendix B.

## Methodology

The headline specifications in Parts I and II are pooled ordinary least squares with year (Part I) or year-window (Part II) fixed effects and standard errors clustered at the state level. The dependent variable and all continuous controls are log-transformed. The coefficient on each control is therefore an elasticity: a 1 percent change in the control is associated with a β-percent change in per-capita digital-payment use, holding the other controls in the column constant. Year fixed effects absorb the period-specific national level so the regression identifies cross-state variation rather than common time trends.

We use four controls in the Part I headline (NSDP, urban share, PMJDY/adult, bank offices) and five in the Part II headline (the same four plus internet density). The build-up tables in Parts I and II add controls one at a time so the marginal contribution of each is visible. The literacy variable is excluded from both headline specifications and treated separately in §9 and the appendix tables.

Sample sizes: Part I has 35 states × 2 fiscal years = 70 state-year observations, of which 66 enter the regression after dropping two states with missing NSDP. Part II has 36 states × 3 windows = 108 state-window observations, of which 99 enter the regression after dropping three states with missing NSDP or capped urban share. Cluster counts (33 in both parts) are at the comfortable side of the standard threshold for cluster-robust inference.

We do not run a within-state specification. With four of the six controls largely time-invariant in the available data (PMJDY snapshots aside, urban share, literacy, internet density move slowly), state fixed effects would absorb most of the variation we are trying to identify. The research design here is cross-state across multiple periods, with year/window FE absorbing common time trends — not a panel-causal design.
